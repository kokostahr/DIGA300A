Published: 16 March 2025
Last updated: 22 May 2025

This blog post is about more than code! Star opens up about going against the easy route of copying tutorials and instead tries to learn JavaScript with intention and awareness. She tries to connected her learning process to decolonial theory, challenging the dominance of Western tech norms in both language and design.

Edit Addition:

In truth, it would be difficult to start decolonising my code, since the languages that I am using are done in English and follow a Western Standard. Even the language I am using on my website is in English and unintentionally excludes users who may not know English that well or at all. It is very ironic of me to say I am taking the steps to decolonise my coding process, but still follow the Western standards set before me when it comes to coding. We may be living in a (perceived) post-colonial world, but many of the world systems and technologies in place stem from the colonial era. This is true regarding the points I mentioned, the approach we are taking to learning coding languages is already quite colonial due to something as simple as the medium of instruction and understanding being English. The way in which we read and write, from left to right, is a Western structure that we have adopted as our own.

South Africa has 11 official languages, yet very few South African owned businesses have ensured that their websites translates to all of them or at least the top 4 languages in the country to ensure accessibility. To truly decolonise my code, I would have to think about the community I left back at home, a community that speaks vernacular languages. How does looking at my website which is fully in English benefit or help them, are they able to understand? If I showed them my code, would they be able to make sense of what I am trying to do even without understanding the concepts? Would I then be able to teach these concepts to them? I need to find a way to bridge that gap. If we look at the ‚ÄòCode kumnandi‚Äô initiative by the CodeMakers, then doing something like this would be the best starting ground. Code kumnandi launched isiZulu as one of the 48 langauages available on the Scratch platform. Scratch is a visual programming language, meant to introduce beginners and children into the world of coding (Internet Matters, 2025).

Localising coding languages into South African home languages would already bridge one part of the gap for many people. Then as developers we would need to consider how we would then either shake the Western structure and ensure that our projects are still taken seriously on the world stage, or bridge the other gap in transferring the knowledge and skills that people built up, so that they‚Äôll have the ability to code in their mother tongue and the language of the world, English.
