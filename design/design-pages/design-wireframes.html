Wireframing Process continued...

by Star Nkosi

Published: 17 March 2025
Last updated: 17 March 2025

In this design post, Star speaks about her wireframing process.

I have already covered the beginning stages of my wireframing process in my blog post for week 2 and week 4. This design section will continue from where I left off in week 4. Please do read it to understand the development of my wireframing process.

Continuing where we left off...

As stated in my blog post for week 4, I used the simplistic design of the blog homepage as an inspiration for the rest of my wireframes. So far I have designed wireframes for each and every page that I want to appear on my website. Some sections, such as the sections for the essays and the portfolio have a basic wireframe layout, that may change slightly as I put the relevant content into it. These basic layouts will act as placeholders to indicate where content will go on each page, within each section. Let's take a look at my wireframes.

Blog Post Wireframes

These wireframes will be used as a guideline for the structure of both the academic and personal blog posts. When creating the wireframes for the blog posts, I already had a few blog posts up on my website. I used the basic HTML structure that I had and the simlistic design of the blog homepage as inspiration while designing these wireframes. In the wireframes I have blocked out space for the blog title, a summary of the blog, the authour and the date and any thoughts I may share before delving into the content.

As I stated before, this wireframe is just an example, a guideline of how I'd like my blogposts to look if they have images or references, but not every blog post will have images and/or references at the end of the post. I want to align the images to the left-hand side of the page, while the text continues alongside it on the right-hand side. This is to save space. I do not like it when there is a big block of empty white space surrounding an image. I also feel like it makes the posts feel 'shorter' since there isn't a large gap in the middle with each photo. The bottom of each blog Post will have a navigation of sorts that allows the user to go to a previous blog post, to the next blog post or back to the blog homepage. This is a navigation-type feature that I've made use of all across my blog to aid with accessibility And intiutive navigation of my website.

There is a concern I have regarding the way I drew my wireframes. Something I only realised towards the end, really. It seems as though I have mistakenly created a fixed footer? I am unsure if this is a thing, but I do not imagine that it would look nice once it has been applied. But in the wireframe design, it does...so it is something I will be considering.

Portfolio Wireframes

The homepage for the portfolio wireframes have a navbar at the top, the side scrolling bar on the right-hand side, and a footer. These elements are maintained throughout my website, on each page for consistency. The way I have structured the portfolio homepage is to showcase previews of the most recent works of my portfolio. This is because I am assuming that anyone who comes to view my skills on my site would want to see my skills at the current stage they're viewing it. There is a small link/button that I have annotated next to the previews of each section of the portfolio. I plan to make this link/button say 'View More'. When the user clicks it, then it will link And redirect them to a page of the relevant section of the portfolio, where they'll be able to explore all my works, from most recent to the earliest works. Allow me to provide an example of what I mean. Let's say I land on the portfolio homepage and am interested in checking out Star's 2D animations. Then I will use the side scrolling bar to scroll down until I spot the "2D Animations" heading. I see three of Star's most recent works, but I'd like to see more. So i click the 'view more' link/button. This link redirects me to a page titled '2D Animations' where I can browse through Star's works.

The image above shows what the relevant sections from the portfolio homepage would look like. As you can see, I've annotated that I want to have a maximum of 8 projects on each page, meaning that I would then have to consider adding a sort of navigation at the bottom of each page that allows users to move to the previous page (within the section), to the next page, to see what page they're on and the option to return to the portfolio homepage. The reason why I have restricted the number of projects shown is because I don't users to have to scroll endlessly to look for a particular post. Truthfully, 8 project posts feels a bit much, since I do plan to have a preview of the project as an image or video, then a summary of it that will give the relevant dates, and details. This is something I will continue to play around with as I add more content to my website.

Commission Wireframe

This wireframe has a very basic blockouk. There is not much that I intend to display on the commission homepage. There is a section for the prices, and a section for the rules associated with commissioning me. Again, this design follows the very neat, simplistic style that I want my website to have. The purpose of this page is straightforward and functional. The way that I have chosen to structure the layout also improves readability and navigation.

Essay Wireframes

As with the commisions homepage, the essay homepage is just as simplistic for effiecient navigation and readability. This essay homepage contains the titles of each academic essay, and a small summary of what the essay is about underneath the title. The summary will provide a "Read more..." link that will redirect the user to the full essay on another page.

When designing the essay page posts, I stuck closely to the design of the portfolio project pages. Except in these wireframes, the navigation at the bottom will display: previous essay (if applicable, e.g. it won't show this on the first essay post.), next essay and back to essay homepage. Adding this navigation improves accessibility. Accessibility is the main reason why I have chosen to have this sort of navigation at the bottom of each post/page/project, as users do not have to scroll all the way up again just to access the navigation bar again.

Design Wireframes

The wireframe for my Design Homepage acts as blueprint for how I envision this page. While my current HTML structure does not exactly match the wireframe layout structure, it still remains similar. The wireframe outlines the different sections that the design homepage will (currently) have.

The design blog/ design post wireframes are very much insipired and follow the same outline as the normal blogpost wireframes. The reason why I chose to make them similar, is because I came to the conclusion that my design section would also be a sort of 'blog post', in the sense that I am still writing and sharing my thoughts and insights. Just like the blog pages, the design pages also have a space for the authour, the date and summary of the post. Again, it is another page on my website that has a mini navigation at the bottom that allows users to move to the prvious post, the next post or back to the homepage.
