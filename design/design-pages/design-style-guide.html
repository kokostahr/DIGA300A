Style Guide

by Star Nkosi

Published: 17 March 2025
Last updated: 14 April 2025

In this design post, Star goes through the her design choices, concerning her style guide. This section is will continuously be updated as changes are made to the style guide, and more interface elements are designed.

Colour Palettes

Something that I want to maintain from my first wireframe designs (where I was exploring the potential colours of my website) would most definetly be the theme of the colour palettle. The name of my site is 'Starfish Galaxy', and I wanted to go for an acquatic theme in space, however, I am unsure of how I would mix colours associated with these two themes into one. So.. for now, I have decided to lean more towards the space theme. I want users to feel like they're browsing through the stars as they explore my site.

A gorgeous colour scheme, if I may say so myself. I believe my colour choices are sophisticate and visually striking, they adhere to the theme, while maintaining readability and accessibility. (PLEASE note that I have not tested how these colours would work together for colour blind users. When I speak of accessibility here, I mean in terms of readability for the users.) Midnight violet (#160649) as the background gives the website an elegant, professional starry night feeling. The royal blue (#296ac8) for the interactive elements, is bold, eye-catching and helps them stand out against the dark background. It makes the site feel lively without being too overwhelming. Lavender Blush (#cba1eb) is used as a soft contrast for the nav bar, scrollbar background and text within the footer. It gives the website a soft 'pop', preventing it from feeling too dark. Soft aqua (#92d2f5) will be used for text to keep things readable against the dark background and the other two saturated colours (also meant to represent the 'stars shining and glowing in the galaxy'). Here is how it would look when laid out:

There is a strong contrast between the colours I have chosen and the dark background. This will keep everything readable and accessible. The interactive elements pop, making sure that users can easily identify the interactive elements. There are some things I am considering though. I am worried that soft aqua wont be 'that' readable on the midnight violet background. In the test image above, it does seem like it will be readable, but I am curious about how it would look on the webpages that are very text heavy, like this webpage for instance.

Honestly, I was worried that my webpage may be a bit jarring, with the whole 'night sky' theme. What inspired me, and convinced me to continue with this idea was Bluefox Studio's Website Their site has that feel of exploring the stars/ galaxy off the bat, without even knowing what the content in the website is. I believe the dark background, and contrasting colours used for the text and other ui elements give it that feel.

Fonts

I have yet to finalise my font choices, but I do want to use fonts that evoke an ethereal yet simplistic feel. The typography should complement my night-sky aesthetic while maintaining readability and a minimalistic, clean design. The reason for having a clean design is because when we look up at the sky, there is cleanliness in the way the stars and things in space shine above our heads. It never feels negatively overwhelming. The sky always feels beautiful, structured and awe inspiring. To mirror this feeling, I am considering fonts like Raleway, Quicksand or Playfair Display as potential options.

Colour Scheme

I am sticking with my current colour scheme, but I need to improve the readability of the colours working together. After analysing and receiving feedback I realised that while the royal blue colour is pretty and matches the overall aesthetic I am going for, it does not contrast well against the darker blue and the purple colour.

However, after adjusting the royal blue colour, I realised that the purple colour could be a bit darker as well, to ensure that the background contrasted with the text.

I tried a lot of different things, such as a gradient background for the navigation bar and footer, and while it looked cool, it also just looked so out of place. When picking new colours for the footer and nav bar, I didn‚Äôt do anything special, I just played with the colour wheel within the css document and saved, checking which options looked the best visually, and used the ‚Äòwave evaluation tool‚Äô to check the contrast until it gave me a good score. The new colours I settled on, still adhere to my theme, while contrasting against one another very, very nicely.

Please note that the footer appears to be a different colour but is still the same colour as the nav bar, I just played with the alpha value to make it slightly different.

Button design

When it came to the buttons, I simply decided to put them into a box, using Moonlit Veil(#f0f8ff) as the border. All the boxes on my website have this colour as the border, because it‚Äôs striking and makes the elements that are contained within it stand out.

I didn‚Äôt formally design my buttons, which was a mistake. I designed them on a whim within my css, settling on the same design for the boxed elements in my webpage. Moonlight Veil (#f0f8ff) as the border colour and Soft Plum(#3f1754) with the alpha turned down as the fill.

UNFORTUNATELY, I couldn‚Äôt decide how I wanted it to look when hovering over it and came up with three options to select from.

The third is the one I have set as the default, because it looks good and maintains consistency, since the hover colour for ‚Äòtext‚Äô links are also yellow. The reason I made the gradient hover buttons was because I feared that the consistency of the yellow hover was a bit too repetitive. I do like the second option, where the white fades into the muted yellow, however, when you look at the webpage in total‚Ä¶it looks very out of place, while at the same time it also fits the aesthetic of the webpage.

In the end, I settled on the third option for buttons within the webpage and on the second option for the ‚ÄòBack To Top‚Äô button.

Folder Structure

I am grateful for all the feedback I received from my lecturers and tutors. My folder structure is good, it eats. But there are a few things that could be improved, such as the naming convention of my images folder. My image folders were called ‚Äúfotos‚Äù, mainly because I thought I was being ‚Äòquirky‚Äô and giving my website naming conventions that were playful. While it was understandable to me, it is not industry standard, and in most cases won‚Äôt be understood by other people within the pipeline of web development. So, I changed the name of all the image folders that have the word ‚Äúfotos‚Äù to images.

Another thing, I was advised to do was to remove the krita and krita autosave files from my website folders because they aren‚Äôt being used directly within my website, and are just sitting, adding confusion and a mess to my structure. I wholeheartedly agreed with that and removed them as advised.

The reason I had put them there initially, was because I planned to draw and create every visual on my website, whether it be images or videos, or a logo, or any other visual, since I did state that I wanted my site to display my skills and be a sort of representation of ‚Äòmy galaxy‚Äô. That is why I kept the krita files within these separate folders within the website, just for ease of saving each image I may make. But they‚Äôve been removed since I don‚Äôt want to overload my website with things that won‚Äôt even be visible.

I was also advised to compress my images, something that I hadn‚Äôt been doing after saving them, especially because I create big, High-Definition images (2970 X 2100) for the higher quality. But after discovering that Krita has an option to compress high quality PNG images, I will make use of that for any future visuals that appear on my website. After a quick google search, I found that the recommended px size for images is 1200 x 800, so when making images and visuals in Krita, I will reduce the canvas size to 1200 x 800.

Ontop of this I was advised to scale down my images, since they were a bit too big, and made each webpage longer than it needed to be.
